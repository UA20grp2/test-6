The Weather Project

Olafur Eliasson's The Weather Project was exhibited at the Tate Modern Museum in Turbine Hall in London in 2003. The installation is a representation of the sun, the sky and a fine mist that hangs in space. The sun is represented by a giant semicircular shape, which consists of hundreds of mono-frequent lamps. Through the mirrors on the ceiling, you see a round shape. The hundreds of lamps combine together to beam a large beam of light that shines all over the hall. On the floor of the hall there is nothing, which invites the audience to participate interactively in the project. Today's cities are thriving in the economy and tourism, but nature is disappearing more and more. According to Elaisson, the weather is one of the few fundamental encounters with nature that can still be experienced in the city. The installation gives attention to how we perceive the world around us. The weather is constantly changing, as are our perceptions. Moreover, the weather is still unpredictable, despite the efforts and sabotage of man. This is in parallel with Eliasson's dynamic project.
